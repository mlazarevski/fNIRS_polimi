[cope1988]
Source : Cope & Delpy, 1988
Used for: foundational MBLL / quantification rationale

> "The advantages of such a system are first that it allows
>  oxygenation changes in the central sections of the brain to be
>  measured; secondly, since the optical path is more closely
>  defined, modelling techniques can be applied to predict the
>  average photon path length, and hence quantitate the measured
>  oxygenation changes."


[delpy1988]
Source : Delpy et al., 1988 — "Estimation of optical pathlength
         through tissue from direct time of flight measurement"
Used for: (1) MBLL foundations
          (2) DPF computed from mean time of flight (Eq. 1 in our
              Methods — Monte Carlo justification)

> "The transit times (t) of the photons can be converted into
>  distance travelled through the medium (d) using the formula
>  d = ct/n where n is the refractive index of the medium and c
>  the velocity of light in a vacuum. For water (n = 1.33) the
>  conversion factor is 0.225 mm/ps and for tissue (n = 1.40)..."

> "A Monte Carlo simulation has shown that the mean of the
>  integrated TPSF is in excellent agreement with the DPF."


[ferrari2012]
Source : Ferrari & Quaresima, NeuroImage, 2012 — review of fNIRS
         history and modalities
Used for: (1) overview of CW / FD / TD modalities
          (2) neurovascular coupling description in Discussion

> "Three different NIRS techniques are used, each based on a
>  specific type of illumination: 1) the CW modality which, based
>  on constant tissue illumination, simply measures light
>  attenuation through the head; 2) the frequency-domain (FD)
>  method which, illuminating the head with intensity-modulated
>  light, measures both attenuation and phase delay of emerging
>  light; and 3) the time-domain (TD) technique which,
>  illuminating the head with short pulses of light, detects the
>  shape of the pulse after propagation through tissues."

> "The increase in O2Hb and the concomitant decrease in HHb
>  reflect an increase in local arteriolar vasodilatation, which
>  increases local cerebral blood flow and cerebral blood volume,
>  a mechanism known as neurovascular coupling. The increased
>  oxygen transported to the area typically exceeds the local
>  neuronal rate of oxygen utilization, resulting in an
>  overabundance of cerebral blood oxygenation in active areas."


[scholkmann2014]
Source : Scholkmann et al., NeuroImage, 2014 — review of CW-fNIRS
         instrumentation and methodology
Used for: comparing fNIRS strengths against EEG / fMRI / MEG

> "The strengths of fNIRI compared to other non-invasive
>  neuroimaging techniques such as EEG, fMRI or MEG include its
>  portability, potential wearability, ease of application, and
>  the low purchase and operation costs (especially when compared
>  to fMRI and MEG), the spatially localized nature of fNIRI in
>  contrast to EEG and the more complete information (O2Hb in
>  addition to HHb) compared to fMRI and its compatibility with
>  other neuroscience techniques."


[scholkmann2013]
Source : Scholkmann & Wolf, J. Biomed. Opt., 2013 — "General
         equation for the differential pathlength factor of the
         frontal human head depending on wavelength and age"
Used for: age- and wavelength-dependent DPF formula
          DPF = alpha + beta*age^gamma + delta*lambda^3
                + epsilon*lambda^2 + zeta*lambda
Status : VERIFIED — but note caveat below

> "We derive a general DPF equation for the frontal human head,
>  incorporating dependency on wavelength and age, based on
>  published data."

CAVEAT: The formula is fitted to FRONTAL-head data only. Our
study targets the OCCIPITAL (visual) cortex. We use it as the
literature reference for lack of a published occipital fit, but
acknowledge this limitation explicitly in Methods/Discussion.

The paper itself cites Delpy et al. 1988 for MBLL, supporting
our co-citation pattern.


[scholkmann2014]
Source : Scholkmann et al., NeuroImage, 2014 — "A review on
         continuous wave functional near-infrared spectroscopy
         and imaging instrumentation and methodology"
Used for: definition of CW-NIRS / NIRI and the [O2Hb]/[HHb]
          quantification rationale (now also a method-context
          citation, not just an introductory one)

> "By shining near-infrared (~650 to 950 nm) light into tissue
>  and measuring the diffuse reflected light at different
>  wavelengths (lambda), continuous-wave (CW) near-infrared
>  spectroscopy (NIRS) and imaging (NIRI) enable the
>  determination of concentration changes in oxy- and
>  deoxyhemoglobin ([O2Hb], [HHb]), which are related to changes
>  in hemodynamics and oxygenation. NIRS refers to a measurement
>  at a single position (i.e., one light-path), whereas NIRI
>  measures simultaneously at different positions."


[tootell1998]
Source : Tootell et al., 1998 — "The retinotopy of visual
         spatial attention"
Used for: V1 retinotopy via wedge-like stimuli
Status : VERIFIED — but flagged as imperfect fit

> "The comparisons between attention to one target versus
>  attention to another showed very clear retinotopy, in the
>  form of increased MR signals at the cortical sites to which
>  the targets projected... The attention-based retinotopy was
>  approximately as precise as that revealed in the
>  (sensory-based) retinotopic maps."

CAVEAT: This paper is primarily about ATTENTION-driven
retinotopy, not the bottom-up sensory drive a flickering
wedge produces. It works as a reference but is not the cleanest
fit. PREFER engel1997 or sereno1995, which used phase-encoded
rotating wedges/rings to map V1 retinotopy via the bottom-up
sensory response — exactly the regime we are in.

[engel1997]   VERIFIED   (replacement for tootell1998)
Source : Engel, Glover, Wandell, Cereb. Cortex, 1997 —
         "Retinotopic organization in human visual cortex and
         the spatial precision of functional MRI"
Used for: V1 retinotopy via phase-encoded ring/wedge stimuli

> "We used a contracting ring stimulus, measured the fMRI
>  signal in a plane parallel to the calcarine sulcus and
>  analyzed data along the linear region of interest within
>  area V1 [...] We expected the stimulus to create a traveling
>  wave of activity moving from anterior to posterior along the
>  region of interest. [...] The fMRI signal is a traveling wave
>  that moves from anterior to posterior portions of cortex."

NOTE: Engel uses contracting RINGS (eccentricity mapping); the
companion phase-encoded WEDGE stimulus maps polar angle. Both
are part of the same retinotopic-mapping family that our
flickering wedge protocol belongs to. The paper establishes
that phase-encoded retinotopic stimuli reliably drive V1 — the
exact claim we need.

[duncan1996]   VERIFIED
Source : Duncan et al., Pediatric Research, 1996 —
         "Measurement of Cranial Optical Path Length as a
         Function of Age Using Phase Resolved Near Infrared
         Spectroscopy"
Used for: (1) literature DPF reference values for adult head
              (n=100 adults, ages 21-59, frontal, SDS=4.3 cm,
              wavelengths 690/744/807/832 nm — see Scholkmann
              2013 table)
          (2) age-dependent DPF equation (precursor to
              Scholkmann 2013)
          (3) [NEW] regional variation citation — see below

> "These values vary with wavelength and age, and have a 12-17%
>  SD comparable to the variation of DPF measured by TOF
>  techniques. The DPF depends upon the proportions of different
>  scatterers and absorbers in tissue [...] Even in a group of
>  similarly aged subjects this may be a source of substantial
>  variation. Other differences include geometrical and anatomical
>  factors such as head shape and optode position. Ferrari et al.
>  (31) have demonstrated an approximately 15% variation in DPF
>  measured by TOF between the frontal and parietal areas of
>  adult foreheads. We have shown a 0.8% variability between
>  repeated measurements at one site on the same subject, which
>  discounts the spread of results being due to machine error."

> "Although from this study we have recommended DPF for use with
>  NIRS measurements in subjects of varying age, it is clear from
>  the data that far better accuracy would be achieved by
>  measuring individual path lengths at the time of each study."

KEY TAKEAWAY: Duncan 1996 explicitly references ~15% variation
in DPF between frontal and parietal areas. THIS is the
load-bearing citation for our caveat that Scholkmann's
frontal-fitted formula (and Duncan's own frontal data) may
under/overestimate occipital DPF in our study. Use it in the
Methods/Discussion when we acknowledge the regional limitation.
